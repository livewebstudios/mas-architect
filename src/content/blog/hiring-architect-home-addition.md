Adding on to your home is one of the most rewarding investments you can make. A new primary suite, a bigger kitchen, a sunroom, a second story — the right addition can transform how your family lives without the upheaval of moving. But for most homeowners, the process is unfamiliar territory, and that can make it feel intimidating. Here's what to expect when you bring an architect on board.

## An Architect Is More Than a Drafter

A contractor can build, but an architect designs. That distinction matters most on an addition, where the new work has to integrate with a house that already exists. An architect studies how your home is put together, how it sits on its lot, and how local code applies — then designs an addition that works structurally, functionally, and aesthetically. The goal is to catch problems on paper, where they cost a pencil to fix, instead of in the field, where they cost real money.

## It Starts With a Conversation

Every project begins with listening. In our first meeting, we want to understand how you live, what's not working in your current home, and what you hope the addition will do for you. We'll also talk candidly about budget — early, not late — so the design stays grounded in reality.

From there, we move into site analysis. We measure and document the existing house, review your survey, and identify the buildable area: the zoning setbacks, lot coverage limits, and any non-conforming conditions that affect what's possible. This feasibility step tells us what you can build before we invest time in what you *want* to build.

## Designing an Addition That Belongs

The best additions don't look like additions. We design new rooflines, window proportions, and materials that read as part of the original home, while improving the flow between old and new. You'll see preliminary floor plans and elevations, and we can generate 3D imagery so you can understand the design and make confident decisions about color, materials, and massing before construction begins.

## Permits and Variances, Demystified

Once the design is set, we compile a complete set of construction documents — the drawings your town needs for permits and your builder needs to build. If your design doesn't meet the town's site limitations because of an existing or newly created condition, a variance may be required. That means a presentation to a local board, and we're happy to guide you through it. It's worth budgeting for the possibility: application fees, attorney fees, additional drawings, printing, and certified mail can all come into play.

## Timeline and What to Prepare

Every project is different, but it helps to think in phases rather than a single deadline. Design typically takes several weeks of back-and-forth; permitting depends on your municipality and whether a variance is involved. You can keep things moving by gathering a few items up front:

- A current survey of your property
- A prioritized wish list — needs versus nice-to-haves
- A realistic budget range
- Photos of homes, rooms, and details you love

## The Payoff

A thoughtfully designed addition does more than add square footage. It resolves the frustrations of your current home, adds long-term value, and lets you stay in the neighborhood you love. With the right architect guiding the process, what feels intimidating at the start becomes a clear, manageable path to a home that finally fits.
